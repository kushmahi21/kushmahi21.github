Two column Design

Can information graphics (infographics) and visualizations1 lie? Most designers and journalists I know would yell a rotund “yes” and rush to present us with examples of outrageously misleading charts and maps. Watchdog organizations such as Media Matters for America have recently began collecting them (Groch-Begley and Shere 2012), and a few satirical Web sites have gained popularity criticizing them.2 Needless to say, they are all great fun

The notion that graphics can indeed lie derives from Darrell Huff’s bestseller How to Lie With Statistics that describes the most common kinds of visual sins, such as truncating the Y-axis of graphs. Edward Tufte, always keen on contriving catchy memes, tried to coat Huff’s teachings with a scientific looking varnish and invented a unit of measurement called the “Lie Factor” (Tufte 1983).
